Microscopy

In order to study sensory processing, my colleague and I created an optical system capable of whole-brain imaging combined with simultaneous optogenetic stimulation. Using this dual system, we examined how the zebrafish brain encodes input from its neuromasts, the sensory cells used by zebrafish to detect water flow.

Voleti et al., 2019

First, I ordered, assembled, aligned, and calibrated all optomechanical components and hardware to build a single-objective light-sheet microscope based on the schematic shown above. Because light-sheet microscopy uses a thin plane of illumination, it has good optical sectioning capabilities and high acquisition speed. Our light-sheet microscope was thus well-suited to image large portions of the zebrafish brain.

Click to show animation. Each bright spot corresponds to a neuromast being targeted with a laser for optogenetic stimulation.

Next, I designed and built a targeted, optogenetic stimulation arm that enabled us to play the individual neuromasts of a zebrafish like the keys of a piano (as shown in the animation above and to the left). We could then deliver complex combinations of neuromast stimulation while simultaneously recording brain activity. Using our combined system (schematized above and shown above and to the right), we studied how neurons encode sensory stimuli.
