Iterative design and fabrication

Photo by Chris Taggart

I have a proven track record of collaborating with scientists to develop the tools needed for their research. Using my skills and experience with additive and subtractive manufacturing, I then design, fabricate, and refine the apparatus as dictated by ongoing results.

As one example, consider the work I did in research on mammalian hearing. Such research is often hindered by the fragility, anatomical inaccessibility, and complexity of the cochlea. In order to overcome these obstacles, we developed a chamber for excised cochlear segments that effectively mimicked the physiological environment of the intact cochlea. This approach also enabled us to fulfill a stringent set of experimental requirements, including stimulation with acoustic pressure, measurement of electrical responses, maintenance of optical access, and ease of pharmacological perturbations.

Some initial designs and iterations of this chamber are shown above and to the left. Results were consistent with an active process that endows hearing with exquisite sensitivity. However, those results were confounded by gravitational effects that altered the natural resting position of the cochlear segment.

In conjunction with my colleagues, I developed a new chamber shown in the animation above that more closely approximated in vivo conditions and reduced gravitational effects. This chamber revealed “a unified biophysical principle that underlies hearing across insects, nonmammalian vertebrates, and mammals alike.” And as stated by Francesco Gianoli, this breakthrough allowed us to “observe the first steps of the hearing process in a controlled way that was previously impossible”.
