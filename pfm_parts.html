Subtractive manufacturing

Human hearing is remarkably sensitive. We can hear sounds as quiet as a mosquito buzzing near one’s ear and as loud as a jet engine. These extremes vary in amplitude by over six orders of magnitude. What elements of our inner ear enable such expansive discrimination?

In order to answer this question, Bartsch et al. designed and built a photonic force microscope (PFM). Similar to optical tweezers, the PFM was used to investigate the role of a protein called protocadherin15.

The PFM is capable of 3D-position measurements with high precision (less than 1 nanometer) and high bandwidth (up to 1 MegaHertz). In order to achieve those stringent benchmarks, all optomechanical components needed to be fabricated precisely and accurately. I made two such components using subtractive manufacturing.

In the schematic shown above and to the left, I machined the part shown in green—a dovetail adapter designed to hold a filter cube. Because the filter cube shown in magenta was proprietary, no drawings were available. I methodically and iteratively worked on the part to ensure a tight and stable fit. The actual piece in the PFM is shown above and to the right and was required to enable fluorescent imaging.

Using Tobias Bartsch’s drawing, I also fabricated the cyan component shown above and to the right. Designed to hold the purple optical prism, this piece was necessary for Differential Interference Contrast (DIC) microscopy, an optical technique that enhances contrast in certain specimens.
